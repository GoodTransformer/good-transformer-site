## 1. AI literacy for leaders is not knowing every AI tool

**LinkedIn (long):**
Most leaders we meet can name more AI tools than they can use well. That is the wrong scoreboard. Tools change every few months; in 2024 US institutions alone released around forty notable AI models. Product knowledge built on that pace is built on sand.

The thing that lasts is judgement: knowing what the technology can do, where it is worth using, how to tell good output from plausible output, and who stays accountable when it gets something wrong. None of that depends on this quarter's favourite app. The EU AI Act now treats a baseline of AI literacy as an organisational duty, defined by role and context rather than which product you have logged into, which is a fair definition for any business to borrow.

The new piece sets out five durable parts of leadership AI literacy and a ten-minute self-assessment to find your weakest one. Worth a read if your team has more logins than understanding.

**LinkedIn (short):**
AI tools change every few months. The judgement to use them well does not. New piece: the five durable parts of AI literacy for leaders, plus a ten-minute self-assessment to find your weakest area. It is about understanding, not collecting logins.

## 2. Assistant, automation or agent: what does your business actually need?

**LinkedIn (long):**
"Agent" is doing a lot of work in sales decks right now. Often the task in front of you would be done better, cheaper and more safely by something far less autonomous, and the only reason to reach for an agent is that the word sounds like the future.

It helps to think in four levels of delegation: Answer (a one-off response), Assist (a reusable helper you drive), Automate (a fixed, predefined workflow) and Act (an agent that decides its own steps). Even Anthropic, which builds these models, advises finding the simplest solution that works and adding autonomy only when needed. Most "agent projects" turn out to be assistants or automations, which is good news: cheaper, faster and far easier to trust.

The new piece gives you the four levels, five tests to apply (autonomy, access, reversibility, risk, human approval), and a one-page decision tree. The goal is not the most autonomous option. It is the lowest level that does the job.

**LinkedIn (short):**
Before you build an "AI agent", check whether the job needs one. Four levels of delegation, Answer, Assist, Automate, Act, and five tests to choose between them. Most agent projects are really assistants or automations: cheaper, safer, easier to trust. New piece, with a one-page decision tree.

**Headlines:**
1. Assistant, automation or agent: what does your business actually need?
2. Most "agent projects" are really automations. That is good news

## 3. How to choose your first serious AI use case

**LinkedIn (long):**
The most common way to choose a first AI project is to pick whatever sounded most impressive in a demo. It is an expensive instinct, because the first project sets what everyone comes to believe AI can do.

The evidence says where you point AI matters more than whether you use it. In a field experiment with 758 consultants, AI lifted performance on tasks suited to it but made people meaningfully worse on a task just outside its range, and confidently so. The unglamorous, frequent, checkable task is usually the better first bet than the futuristic one.

The new piece gives you the VALUE test, Value, Activity, Learnability, Uncertainty, Ease, and a scorecard to compare up to five candidate use cases, with warning flags for the seductive, brittle project that scores high only on ambition. Choosing well is unglamorous, and it is most of the result.

The best first AI project is rarely the most futuristic one. It is the frequent, checkable, low-stakes task where a mistake is cheap. New piece: the VALUE test (Value, Activity, Learnability, Uncertainty, Ease) and a scorecard to compare your candidates and spot the brittle ones.

## 4. AI productivity is not the same as business value

**LinkedIn (long):**
A team tells you AI saves them six hours a week. It is the kind of number that ends a meeting happily. The harder question is: and then what? Where did those hours go, and did anything the business cares about actually move?

Most of the time, nobody can answer, and that is the gap at the centre of AI adoption. A task-level time saving is real, but it does not travel up to the business on its own. It has to climb four rungs: task efficiency, workflow improvement, capacity release, and business outcome. Value leaks at every step you skip. The freed time does not redeploy itself; left alone, it quietly refills with more of the same work.

The new piece gives you a worksheet to trace one saving up the four rungs, and the two questions that decide whether it becomes value: what happens to the freed time, and how will you measure the outcome. Most teams answer neither.

**LinkedIn (short):**

## 5. Your team's AI experiments are not an AI strategy

**LinkedIn (long):**
Walk through most organisations and you find AI everywhere and nowhere: someone drafting with ChatGPT, an analyst with a Copilot habit, a partner running documents through Claude. It feels like momentum. A business with thirty people each using AI their own way does not have a strategy; it has thirty experiments and a rising sense that something is happening.

Experiments are good, but a pile of local efficiencies is not a direction, and the evidence shows scattered use hitting a ceiling: high adoption, thin measured results. A strategy does not need to be a long document. It needs to answer seven questions in order: business objective, priority workflows, capabilities and tools, data and knowledge, human responsibilities, measures of value, and review.

The new piece lays out that stack and gives you a one-page canvas a leadership team can fill in a single session. The job is not to stop the experimenting. It is to harvest it into a decision, so the learning compounds instead of evaporating when one enthusiast leaves.

## 6. A practical 90-day AI plan for a small business

**LinkedIn (long):**
Most AI advice aimed at small businesses is borrowed from large ones: transformation programmes, centres of excellence, multi-year roadmaps. None of it fits an organisation of fifteen or fifty people with no spare capacity and no patience for theatre.

A smaller business needs a short, controlled sequence instead. Ninety days is the right unit: long enough to change how one or two pieces of work actually get done, short enough that nobody loses interest before there is something to show. Days 1 to 30, understand: objectives, current use, risks, candidate workflows, a baseline. Days 31 to 60, build: pick one or two use cases, create reusable instructions, test with a small group including a sceptic, set human approval. Days 61 to 90, adopt: train on real examples, formalise, measure, assign ownership, then stop, improve or scale.

The new piece includes a week-by-week planner with owners, review points and a stop-or-scale decision built in. Ninety honest days beat a three-year programme nobody finishes.

**LinkedIn (short):**
A small business does not need a three-year AI transformation. It needs ninety controlled days: understand, build, adopt. New piece, with a week-by-week planner that has owners, review points and a stop-or-scale decision built in. Evidence and momentum, not theatre.

## 7. How to write an AI policy employees will actually use

**LinkedIn (long):**
Most AI policies fail in one of two ways. Either they do not exist, and staff quietly paste client data into whatever tool they fancy, or they run to twenty pages nobody reads. Both leave a business exposed.

The useful target sits between them: short and specific enough that an ordinary employee can hold it in their head and apply it on a Tuesday afternoon. We use seven lines: approved tools, information that must never be entered, permitted and prohibited uses, required checking, human approval points, recording and disclosure, and where to ask for help. Each is one plain decision with a real example beside it. The regulators point the same way: the ICO is clear that data-protection duties apply in full to AI, and the NCSC advises allowing tool access deliberately.

The new piece includes an editable template with example wording and scenario boxes for the awkward cases. It is a practical starting point, not legal advice, and a page people can quote beats a binder they ignore.

**LinkedIn (short):**
A short, specific AI policy protects a business better than a long one nobody reads. Seven plain lines, from approved tools to where to ask for help, that an employee can actually remember. New piece, with an editable template and scenario boxes. Not legal advice; a usable starting point.

## 8. Why intelligent people can become slower with AI

**LinkedIn (long):**
A finding worth sitting with. In a 2025 randomised trial, experienced developers expected AI to speed them up by 24%. It slowed them by 19%. And they still believed it had sped them up. They were wrong about the benefit, wrong about its direction, and did not notice.

AI does not just do a task; it adds steps around it. You explain what you want, inspect what comes back, carry the cost of subtle errors, and correct them. For a genuine expert on familiar work, that overhead can outweigh the gain, which fits the wider evidence that AI lifts the floor more than the ceiling.

This is not "AI is useless". It is far more useful: the benefit varies sharply by task and person, and the feeling of speed is an unreliable guide. The new piece gives you a six-part friction test and a simple time log to check, on your own work, where AI genuinely helps and where it quietly costs you. Trust the clock over the feeling.

## 9. The AI skills gap starts at the top

**LinkedIn (long):**
"AI skills gap" almost always points downward, at the front line, the staff who need training. The evidence points the other way. In one large survey, 78% of AI users were already bringing their own tools to work, while 60% of leaders said their company had no AI plan. The workforce is not the bottleneck. The direction is.

Employees cannot adopt AI intelligently in a vacuum. When no one has said what AI is for, what is permitted, or how success is judged, people improvise privately, and the energy never adds up. Closing the gap is four leadership obligations: establish direction, model responsible use, create permission to experiment, and recognise and spread what works. None require a leader to be technical.

The new piece includes a scored readiness checklist for a leadership team to turn those obligations on themselves before turning anything on the staff. The honest answers are usually uncomfortable, which is exactly why they are useful.

## 10. Every AI agent needs a job description

**LinkedIn (long):**
When a business brings someone into an operational role, it writes a job description: remit, limits, who they report to, what they escalate. Then it deploys an AI agent to do comparable work, gives it access to live systems and the freedom to act, and writes nothing. It just switches it on.

That asymmetry is where agent trouble starts. An agent is not a feature; it is a delegated role, and with an agent you are handing over not just the task but the judgement about how to do it. So it needs the same scaffolding a role gets: purpose, tasks, the tools and data it can reach, what it may do alone, what needs approval, a quality standard, escalation, logging, a named human owner, and a way to switch it off.

The new piece includes a one-page agent job card and a completed example. The gaps you cannot fill in are precisely your exposure: a power with no approval step, a system it can reach that nobody decided it should.

**LinkedIn (short):**
You would not give a new hire access and authority with no job description. Do not give an AI agent one either. An agent is a delegated role: it needs a remit, limits, a quality bar, escalation and a named owner. New piece, with a one-page job card and a worked example.

## 11. What leaders should never delegate to AI

**LinkedIn (long):**
AI can now draft the redundancy letter, rank the applicants, and price the contract. Because it clearly can contribute to these decisions, it becomes tempting to let it make them. Capability slides into authority without anyone deciding that it should.

The question is not whether AI can help with a decision. It nearly always can. It is whether the final responsibility can safely be separated from the person who is answerable for it. A model cannot be accountable; when an AI-assisted decision goes wrong, the responsibility sits exactly where it always did. We use a five-part test, HUMAN: High consequence, Unclear or contested, Morally or relationally significant, Accountable to law or customers or employees, and Not readily reversible. The more a decision triggers, the more firmly it stays with a person.

The new piece includes a checklist that sorts a decision into green, amber or red. None of this keeps AI out of important work. It draws the line on who decides, not on how much AI contributes up to that point.

**LinkedIn (short):**
AI can inform almost any decision. That is not the same as it making the decision. New piece: the HUMAN test for the calls that must stay with a person, High consequence, Unclear, Moral, Accountable, Not reversible, with a green/amber/red checklist. The line is on who is answerable, not on how much AI helps.

**Headlines:**
1. What leaders should never delegate to AI
2. The HUMAN test for decisions that stay with a person
3. AI can inform the decision. It cannot hold the responsibility

**Pull quote:** "AI can inform the decision. It cannot hold the responsibility."

**Newsletter intro:** Some decisions can take all the AI help available and still must be made by a person. This piece offers a five-part test, HUMAN, and a checklist to tell which ones, and why the line falls on responsibility.

## 12. Shadow AI is not just a security problem

**LinkedIn (long):**
When IT finds staff using AI tools nobody approved, the instinct is to lock it down. That instinct is not wrong, and on its own it is not enough. In one survey, 78% of AI users were bringing their own tools, rising to 80% in smaller firms. That is not a fringe of rule-breakers; it is most of your AI users, telling you something.

Handle the security risk, then read the signal. Shadow AI usually carries four messages: a workflow is frustrating, employees believe AI can help, your approved provision is inadequate or unknown, and there is no safe route to experiment. A ban that stops at "stop doing that" fixes the leak and learns nothing, while teaching people to hide the next useful thing they find.

The new piece includes an anonymous discovery survey to surface what is really in use, which experiments are working, and where sensitive data is at risk, written to get honest answers. Treat shadow AI as feedback, not just as a crime.

## 13. AI training fails when the workflow stays the same

**LinkedIn (long):**
The workshop goes well. People leave able to do things they could not that morning. Three weeks later, the team is working exactly as before. This is the normal outcome of AI training, and the training was usually fine. The problem is what did not happen around it.

Training changes what a person can do. It does not, by itself, change the approvals, incentives, data access and quality checks around the work, so the old way stays the path of least resistance. Adoption is a property of the workflow, not the headcount you have trained. The fix is to treat training as one step of four: Learn, Apply, Redesign, Reinforce. The third, redesigning the workflow so the new way is the easy way, is the one almost everyone skips, because it is harder than booking a trainer.

The new piece includes a 30-day plan to turn one lesson into a changed workflow, with the redesign and reinforcement built in. If you are not willing to redesign, spend the training budget elsewhere.

Most AI training fades in weeks, not because it was bad, but because nothing around the work changed. Train the person, leave the workflow untouched, and the old way wins. New piece: Learn, Apply, Redesign, Reinforce, with a 30-day plan to make one lesson actually stick.

## 14. AI can give one person the output of a team

**LinkedIn (long):**
A single capable person with AI can now draft the campaign, build the deck, run the analysis and write the code that used to need four people. It leads quickly to a conclusion that is half right and wholly dangerous: that you simply need fewer people.

What actually changes is where a team adds value, not whether it does. AI is very good at producing a team's worth of output. It is not good at supplying a team's worth of challenge and scrutiny, which is what stops confident work from being confidently wrong. So the management move is to choose a mode on purpose: solo plus AI for reversible, lower-stakes work; expert plus AI where judgement matters; team plus AI where the work is contested or high-consequence.

The new piece includes a selector to make that call. The new failure mode is running team-grade work as solo-plus-AI just because the tool made it possible: fast, polished, and unchallenged, which is the most expensive kind of wrong.

**LinkedIn (short):**
AI lets one person produce what used to need a team. It does not produce a team's judgement. New piece: three modes, solo, expert and team, each with AI, and how to choose. The new failure mode is running contested, high-stakes work solo just because the tool now lets you.

## 15. AI adoption is rising. Why is business impact still uncertain?

**LinkedIn (long):**
Every survey says AI use is up sharply. Then comes the quieter boardroom question: so where are the results? Adoption has outrun impact, and the gap is not a mystery. It is a ladder, and most organisations have climbed only the first rung.

Value has to climb four steps, with a different thing blocking it at each: access without competence, competence without workflow integration, integration without measurement, and measurement without strategic reinvestment. Most organisations think they have a vague "AI problem" when they have a specific rung problem, and the wrong fix for your rung is wasted effort. Buying another tool adds to the bottom rung, where everyone already has plenty, while the bottleneck sits two rungs up.

The new piece, the capstone of our series, includes a maturity ladder to locate yourself honestly and the next move for each rung. The leaders pulling ahead are not the ones who bought the most. They treated adoption as a sequence to climb, not a purchase to make.
