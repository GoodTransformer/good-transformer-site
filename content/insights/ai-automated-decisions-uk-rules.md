If your firm uses AI to screen job applicants, score candidates, or sort clients by risk, the UK rules that govern those decisions changed on 5 February 2026, and most firms have not noticed. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 reworked the part of data protection law that covers significant decisions made about people by a machine. It loosened the previous default prohibition, which is welcome if you want to use these tools. It also left one condition standing: where a human is meant to be checking the machine's call, that check has to be real. The regulator has already looked at recruitment, and its evidence suggests many firms overestimate how real their human review is.

One thing to fix in place first. This is about significant, solely automated decisions about identifiable people, based on their personal data: the kind that decide who gets hired, shortlisted, or refused. It does not cover every automated workflow, every AI suggestion a person signs off, or decisions about a company rather than a person. And what follows is general information, not legal advice. The aim is to know what changed and where to look, not to settle your own firm's position, which is worth proper advice.

This is a UK story, and worth being precise about. The headlines this spring have focused on the EU AI Act and its deadlines, which matter for some firms here depending on [whether the Act reaches them at all](/insights/eu-ai-act-and-your-firm/). The change that lands directly on a firm based in the UK is the domestic one, and it has had far less attention.

## What actually changed

For years, Article 22 of the UK GDPR governed solely automated decisions about a person that carry a legal or similarly significant effect: decisions made by a machine with no meaningful human involvement. Being refused credit or rejected for a job is the kind of thing it covers. Being filtered out of a shortlist can also count, where it materially affects someone's chance of the role and no person was meaningfully involved. The old default was a prohibition. Unless one of a small number of gateways applied, usually explicit consent, the necessity of a contract, or authorisation by law, the decision was not allowed.

The Data (Use and Access) Act replaced that single article with a new set, Articles 22A to 22D, in force from 5 February 2026. The default flips. Outside special category data, a significant solely automated decision is no longer treated as forbidden unless it fits a narrow exception. It can be made where the firm has a valid lawful basis and the required safeguards are in place. As the [ICO explains for organisations](https://ico.org.uk/about-the-ico/what-we-do/legislation-we-cover/data-use-and-access-act-2025/the-data-use-and-access-act-2025-what-does-it-mean-for-organisations/), the change opens up a wider set of reasons a firm can rely on, so long as it applies appropriate safeguards. For a recruiter who wanted to use a scoring tool but kept tripping over the old consent requirement, that is a real easing.

There is one carve-out that matters, and it is the sensitive one. The easing does not apply in the same way where a decision is based, wholly or partly, on special category data: health, racial or ethnic origin, religious or philosophical beliefs, and the rest of that protected set. That includes cases where a tool infers or uses such a trait in deciding whether a candidate progresses, even if the inference is wrong. Those decisions sit under a tighter set of conditions, and they are exactly where the legal detail is worth advice rather than guesswork.

## The condition that bites: meaningful human involvement

The easing comes with a test, and the test is where firms are getting caught. Meaningful human involvement is not a box you tick after the fact. It is part of the legal test for whether a decision counts as solely automated at all. If the involvement is not meaningful, the decision is solely automated, and the heavier duties apply.

In March 2026 the ICO published [Recruitment Rewired](https://ico.org.uk/about-the-ico/what-we-do/recruitment-rewired/), drawing on evidence from more than thirty employers who spoke to it voluntarily between March 2025 and January 2026. Its key finding was that many employers running automated recruitment are likely making solely automated decisions without realising it, because the human involvement they rely on is not meaningful. This came from voluntary engagement rather than an audit, but the message to employers is plain.

The bar the ICO sets is higher than the comfortable version most firms tell themselves. A person counts as meaningfully involved only where they can exercise real influence over the decision before it is applied, with the authority, the discretion and the competence to change it. A reviewer handed a ranked shortlist, who signs it off without genuinely being able to overturn it, is not meaningfully involved in the decision that mattered, which was the one to exclude everyone else.

Picture the common case. A firm receives eight hundred applications and runs them through a scoring tool. The tool surfaces the top forty, and a hiring manager picks a shortlist from those. If the manager never sees the seven hundred and sixty the tool set aside, and has no realistic way to revisit the scoring, the decision that mattered was the machine's. That is the gap the ICO is pointing at, and it is an easy one to fall into without noticing.

## Where this reaches, beyond hiring

Recruitment is the clearest case, which is why the ICO started there. CV filtering, suitability scoring, online assessments and behavioural analysis can fall squarely in scope, where they evaluate personal data and decide who progresses or is rejected without meaningful human involvement. Not every automated screen is caught: a simple yes or no check against a human-set essential requirement, such as the right to work in the UK, can be different.

The logic is not limited to hiring. The same rule can reach any significant, solely automated decision about an identifiable person, based on their personal data. A firm that scores new clients for onboarding risk, or runs automated eligibility or affordability checks, may be in scope where those decisions materially affect identifiable individuals, such as sole traders, directors, guarantors or applicants, rather than companies in the abstract.

## What to do this quarter

The practical response is short, and most of it is not legal work. Find where AI touches a consequential decision about a person across the firm: hiring, onboarding, eligibility, risk. For each one, ask the plain question the new rules turn on. Could the person in the loop realistically change the outcome, or are they signing off what the machine has already decided? Where you are not sure, treat that as the signal to look closer and take proper advice, rather than to assume the old "a human approves it" story still holds.

At a high level, the law now expects two things of a firm making these decisions: a human check that is genuine, and basic fairness towards the person, which includes telling them when an automated decision is being made and giving them a way to ask for a human to look again. How those duties apply to your particular tools, data and decisions is exactly the kind of thing to take advice on, not to settle from a blog. The job for a leader is narrower and more useful: know which of the firm's decisions a machine is really making, so you can put a person who can change the outcome where it counts, and ask the right questions of your advisers and your vendors.

## Home ground, and where the UK differs from the EU

It helps to be clear about which rules are actually yours. The UK has no single AI Act equivalent to the EU's. The legal duties for a firm here sit mainly in the UK GDPR as amended by the Data (Use and Access) Act, the Data Protection Act 2018 and the Equality Act 2010, with ICO guidance showing how the regulator reads those duties rather than adding new law of its own. The Equality Act point is easy to forget: a screening process that disadvantages a protected group can create real risk under that Act, and the firm carries that risk even when the tool came from a vendor.

The UK has loosened its automated-decision default for ordinary, non-sensitive data, while the EU has gone the other way, layering high-risk duties on hiring AI through its AI Act and keeping the stricter decision rule. So a firm that hires or serves people in the EU may have EU obligations on top, depending on scope, and the looser UK position is not the one that governs that EU-facing work. Knowing which regime applies to which part of the business is the judgement worth getting right.

## FAQ

### Did the UK ban or allow AI to screen candidates and score clients?

Neither outright. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 moved significant, solely automated decisions about people from a default prohibition under the old Article 22 to a permitted-with-safeguards regime under the new Articles 22A to 22D, in force from 5 February 2026. Such decisions can now be made where the firm has a valid lawful basis and proper safeguards are in place. Where the decision is based wholly or partly on special category data, such as health or ethnicity, tighter conditions apply. How this works for a specific firm is a matter for legal advice.

### What counts as meaningful human involvement under the new UK rules?

The ICO's test is that a person can exercise real influence over the decision before it is applied, with the authority, discretion and competence to change it. A reviewer who signs off a tool's ranked output without being genuinely able to overturn it is not meaningfully involved, and the decision is then treated as solely automated, with the fuller duties that brings.

### Does the new regime apply to AI used in hiring?

It can. Recruitment is where the ICO has focused first. CV filtering, suitability scoring, online assessments and candidate ranking can involve solely automated decisions where the tool evaluates personal data, the effect on the person is significant, and there is no meaningful human involvement. In its March 2026 Recruitment Rewired report the ICO found many employers were likely making such decisions without realising it.

### What should a firm do first?

Map where AI touches a consequential decision about a person, across hiring, client onboarding, eligibility and risk. For each, ask honestly whether the human in the loop could realistically change the outcome. Where the answer is no, or unclear, treat it as a flag to review the process and take proper advice, rather than assuming the previous "a human approves it" position still applies.
