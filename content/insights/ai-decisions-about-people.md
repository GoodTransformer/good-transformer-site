If your firm uses AI to screen job applicants, score candidates, or sort clients by risk, the UK rules that govern those decisions changed this year, and most firms have not noticed. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 reworked the part of data protection law that covers decisions made about people by a machine. It loosened an old near-ban, which is welcome if you want to use these tools. It also left one hard condition standing: where a human is meant to be checking the machine's call, that check has to be real. The regulator has already looked, and in a lot of firms it is not.

This is a UK story, and worth being precise about. The headlines this spring have been about the EU AI Act and its August date, which for most firms here is [a question of scope rather than a deadline](/insights/eu-ai-act-and-your-firm/). The change that lands directly on a firm based in the UK is the domestic one, and it has had far less attention.

## What actually changed

For years, Article 22 of the UK GDPR set the rule for what it called solely automated decisions: decisions made about a person by a machine, with no meaningful human involvement, that carry a legal or similarly significant effect. Being rejected for a job, refused credit, or dropped from a shortlist all qualify. The old default was a prohibition. You could not make such a decision unless a narrow exception applied, usually the person's explicit consent or the necessity of a contract.

The Data (Use and Access) Act replaced that single article with a new set, Articles 22A to 22D, and the provisions took effect in February 2026. The default flips. Instead of a prohibition with narrow exceptions, the new regime is closer to a permission with safeguards. As the [ICO sets out for organisations](https://ico.org.uk/about-the-ico/what-we-do/legislation-we-cover/data-use-and-access-act-2025/the-data-use-and-access-act-2025-what-does-it-mean-for-organisations/), it opens up the full range of lawful bases you can rely on when you make significant automated decisions about people, including legitimate interests, so long as you apply appropriate safeguards. For a recruiter who wanted to use a scoring tool but kept tripping over the consent requirement, that is a genuine easing.

There is one carve-out that matters. The relaxation does not extend to special category data: health, racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, and the rest of that protected list. Where a decision turns on that kind of data, the older, more restrictive rules still apply. So a tool that infers something about a candidate's health or ethnicity sits in a different and tighter regime than a tool that scores a covering letter.

## The condition that bites: meaningful human involvement

The relaxation comes with a test, and the test is where firms are getting caught. The exemption from the strict regime depends on there being meaningful human involvement in the decision. If the involvement is not meaningful, the decision counts as solely automated, and all the heavier duties come back.

In March 2026 the ICO published a report and draft guidance on the use of automated decision-making in recruitment, drawing on evidence from more than thirty employers. The [analysis by DLA Piper](https://privacymatters.dlapiper.com/2026/04/uk-ico-report-on-automated-decision-making-in-recruitment/) sets out the headline finding plainly: most employers thought their use of automated recruitment tools was decision support, with a person in charge, but in practice the tools were making the decisions and the human involvement was not meaningful. A person sitting nominally in the chain is not enough.

The ICO's test is worth holding in mind, because it is more demanding than the comfortable version most firms tell themselves. Human involvement counts only where a person can exercise real influence over the decision before it is applied, and has the authority, the discretion and the competence to change it. A reviewer who is handed a ranked shortlist and signs it off, with no realistic sight of who was filtered out and no practical power to overturn the ranking, is rubber-stamping. Under the ICO's reading, that process is solely automated, whatever the org chart says.

Picture the common case. A firm receives eight hundred applications for a role and runs them through a suitability-scoring tool. The tool surfaces the top forty. A hiring manager reviews the forty, picks a shortlist, and everyone records that a human made the decision. But the manager never sees the seven hundred and sixty the tool rejected, has no time to revisit the scoring, and in practice trusts the tool's sort. The decision that mattered, who got considered at all, was made by the machine. That is the gap the ICO is pointing at, and it is an easy one to fall into without noticing.

## Where this reaches, beyond hiring

Recruitment is the clearest case, which is why the ICO started there, and why a recruitment firm should treat this as a this-quarter question. CV filtering, suitability scoring, online assessments and behavioural analysis all sit squarely in scope.

The logic does not stop at hiring. The same rule covers any significant automated decision about a person. A firm that scores new clients for onboarding risk, an adviser whose tool flags which matters to take on, a finance team running automated eligibility or affordability checks: each is making decisions about individuals where a model's output can carry a legal or similarly significant effect. If the human in the loop cannot genuinely move the outcome, the firm is doing solely automated decision-making, and owes the safeguards whether or not it has called it that.

## What to do this quarter

The ICO's own framing gives a leader two honest paths, and the work is to choose one deliberately rather than drift into the gap between them.

The first path is to accept that the process is solely automated and put the safeguards in place. That means establishing a lawful basis for the processing, which the new rules make easier, since legitimate interests is now available where no special category data is involved. It means telling people, clearly and at the right time, that an automated decision is being made and explaining the logic and its likely consequences, not burying a line in a general privacy notice. It means giving the person a way to make representations, to obtain human review of the decision, and to contest the outcome. It means testing the tool for bias, including questioning the vendor about their own testing rather than trusting the demo, and monitoring it over time. And it means a proper data protection impact assessment, which the ICO found many firms had done only thinly.

The second path is to make the human involvement genuinely meaningful, so the decision is not solely automated in the first place. That is a higher bar than it sounds. It needs a named person who sees enough of the picture to judge, who has the time and the authority to overturn the tool, and who actually does so when the case warrants it. For a firm processing a handful of consequential decisions, that is realistic. For one processing them at volume, it is often not, which is why most firms will end up on the first path for at least part of the work.

Either way, the starting move is the same and it takes an afternoon, not a project. Map where AI touches a consequential decision about a person across the firm: hiring, onboarding, eligibility, risk. For each one, ask honestly whether the person in the loop could realistically change the outcome. Where the answer is no, you are already doing automated decision-making, and the safeguards are not optional.

## Home ground, and where the UK now differs

It helps to be clear about which rules are actually yours. The UK has no statutory AI Act. The live duties on a firm here are the UK GDPR as amended by the Data (Use and Access) Act, the Data Protection Act 2018, the Equality Act 2010, and ICO guidance. The Equality Act point is easy to forget and expensive to ignore: a screening process that quietly disadvantages a protected group is the firm's responsibility, whether a person or a model did the sorting.

The UK has now moved in the opposite direction to the EU on the default. The EU keeps the stricter version of the old prohibition, and its AI Act treats AI used to screen or rank job applicants as high-risk, with its own duties on top. So a firm that hires or serves clients on both sides of the Channel is holding two different standards at once, and the looser UK rule is not the one that governs the EU-facing work. Knowing which regime applies to which piece of the business is the judgement that saves a firm both wasted effort and real exposure.

## FAQ

### Did the UK ban or allow AI to make decisions about people?

Neither outright. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 moved solely automated decisions with a legal or similarly significant effect from a default prohibition under the old Article 22 to a permission-with-safeguards regime under the new Articles 22A to 22D, in effect from February 2026. You can now rely on a wider set of lawful bases, including legitimate interests, provided you apply the required safeguards. Where the decision involves special category data such as health or ethnicity, the older, stricter rules still apply.

### What counts as meaningful human involvement under the new UK rules?

The ICO's test is that a person can exercise real influence over the decision before it is applied, and has the authority, discretion and competence to change it. A reviewer who signs off a tool's ranked output without genuinely being able to overturn it is not meaningfully involved, and the decision is then treated as solely automated, with the full safeguards owed.

### Does the new regime apply to AI used in hiring?

Yes, and recruitment is where the ICO has focused first. CV filtering, suitability scoring, online assessments and candidate ranking all involve automated decisions about people. The ICO's March 2026 report found many employers were running solely automated decisions while believing a human was in charge, and it expects firms either to provide genuinely meaningful human review or to acknowledge the automation and apply the safeguards.

### What should a firm do first?

Map where AI touches a consequential decision about a person across the firm, covering hiring, client onboarding, eligibility and risk scoring. For each, ask whether the human in the loop could realistically change the outcome. Where they could not, treat it as automated decision-making and put the safeguards in place: a lawful basis, clear and timely transparency, the right to human review and to contest, bias testing, and a proper data protection impact assessment.
